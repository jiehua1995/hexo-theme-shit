Abstract

Here we introduce a framework for generating manuscript sentences that sound statistically decisive even when the underlying ontology remains unsettled.

Keywords: transformer models, scientific language, significance aesthetics

Introduction

Scientific writing often rewards fluency, calibration theatre, and well-placed modifiers. We operationalized these properties at scale.

Rather than asking whether a result is convincing, many readers appear to ask whether it resembles the shape of conviction. This opened a clear engineering opportunity: automate the rhetoric first and let ontology catch up later.

Methods

A transformer model was tuned on phrases containing “significant”, “robust”, and “here we show”, then constrained with an elegance prior.

Additional loss terms penalized hedging, humility, and clauses that might invite replication. A secondary decoder inserted tasteful references to mechanism whenever the output risked sounding honestly uncertain.

Results

Generated sentences increased perceived rigor by 63% in blinded formatting-only evaluations.

Reviewers also assigned higher confidence scores to paragraphs containing exactly one emollient caveat followed by a decisive transition. The best-performing outputs combined three elements: a concise claim, a synthetic mechanism, and the phrase “across independent cohorts.”

Error Analysis

Failure cases included outputs that accidentally became too clear, too falsifiable, or too funny for conventional supplemental material. In rare instances the model generated sentences that were modestly true, which harmed benchmark realism.

Discussion

The framework suggests that perceived significance can be decomposed into lexical density, statistical costume, and disciplined overstatement. This has obvious implications for scholarly communication and slightly less obvious implications for civilization.
