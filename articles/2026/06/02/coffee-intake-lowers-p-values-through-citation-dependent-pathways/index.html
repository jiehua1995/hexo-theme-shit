Abstract

Here we report that caffeine exposure appears to lower p-values, especially when manuscripts contain sufficient ceremonial citations.

Introduction

Coffee is widely believed to improve alertness. Less appreciated is its rumored ability to refine inferential thresholds.

Among deadline-season populations, caffeine appears to alter not only wakefulness but also the emotional plausibility of borderline findings. Investigators frequently report that hypotheses become more elegant around cup three and more inevitable around cup five.

Methods

Repeated observational sessions were performed between 01:00 and 04:00 with escalating espresso concentrations and weakening methodological restraint.

Citation density was monitored in real time by counting how many parenthetical references could be inserted before a sentence collapsed under its own authority. A panic-adjusted significance model was fit using napkin-based notation and one increasingly judgmental spreadsheet.

Results

Each additional cup was associated with lower p-values, denser citations, and stronger belief in marginal findings.

The steepest effect was observed when caffeine exposure coincided with reviewer comments containing the phrase “please clarify.” Under those conditions, authors displayed dramatic increases in table production, mechanistic storytelling, and faith in subgroup analyses.

Discussion

The mechanism may involve tremor-assisted confidence or deadline-induced magical thinking.

We cannot rule out reverse causation, wherein weak findings provoke coffee consumption, which then encourages more interpretive aerobics, which then retrospectively justifies the coffee. This loop is common in naturalistic manuscript habitats.

Translational Outlook

If replicated, the findings may inform humane caffeine guidelines for research groups, though no participant requested fewer stimulants. Several instead requested larger mugs and better legends for supplementary figure panels.
