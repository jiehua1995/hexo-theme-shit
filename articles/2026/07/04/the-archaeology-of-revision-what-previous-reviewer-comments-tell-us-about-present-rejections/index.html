A stratigraphic analysis of revision history

Abstract

Here we develop a stratigraphic framework for analyzing the layers of reviewer comments that accumulate on a manuscript through successive revision cycles, discovering that each revision layer encodes the intellectual priorities of a different time, a different reviewer, and a different editorial vision.

Keywords: revision history, reviewer comments, stratigraphy

Introduction

Every revised manuscript is a palimpsest: a document written, erased, and rewritten multiple times, with traces of earlier versions visible beneath the surface. This paper applies archaeological methodology to the study of revision layers.

The Stratigraphic Layers

Layer Origin Characteristics
L0 (Original) Author’s first draft Confident, naive, structurally sound
L1 (Rev 1) Reviewer 1 + Reviewer 2 Defensive additions, new analyses
L2 (Rev 2) Editor + Reviewer 2 Structural rearrangement, humility insertion
L3 (Rev 3) Editor (sympathetic) Minimal changes, “looks great now”
L4+ Subsequent journals Different reviewers, same fate

The Palimpsest Effect

As revision layers accumulate, the manuscript becomes increasingly difficult to read not because it is poorly written but because it has been edited by multiple hands with different priorities, creating a text that is internally inconsistent, overqualified, and structurally incoherent.

Discussion

The archaeology of revision reveals that a “revised and resubmitted” manuscript is not the same intellectual object as the original. It is a palimpsest — a document shaped as much by rejection and revision as by the author’s original intention. Reading the revision history is like reading the emotional biography of a paper.
