Abstract

Here we track the emotional and attitudinal trajectory of academics serving as peer reviewers over 15 years, documenting the transition from eager intellectual engagement to cynical obligation.

Introduction

Peer review is officially described as a civic duty performed with intellectual enthusiasm. This paper documents the actual emotional trajectory of reviewers over their careers.

The Five Stages of Reviewer Development

Stage 1: Enthusiastic Engagement (Years 1-3)

“Thank you for this opportunity! I will read every word carefully and provide thorough, constructive feedback. I am honored to contribute to the scholarly conversation.”

Stage 2: Pragmatic Realism (Years 4-7)

“I will read the abstract and conclusions first to decide how much effort to invest. I will focus on major issues and mention minor ones only if I am feeling generous.”

Stage 3: Strategic Editing (Years 8-12)

“I have developed shortcuts. I know which methods sections to skim and which to read. I have a template for ‘minor revision’ comments. I am efficient.”

Stage 4: Cynical Efficiency (Years 13-15)

“I will accept the paper if it has data and a conclusion. I will suggest changes that require no work. I will write ‘the authors have addressed my concerns’ even when they have not.”

Stage 5: Reviewer Retirement

“I am too busy to review. Please excuse me from the editorial board. (I am not too busy.)”

Discussion

The reviewer career trajectory demonstrates that peer review is not a stable institution but a process of moral and intellectual decay that transforms every enthusiastic academic into a cynical bureaucrat given enough time.
