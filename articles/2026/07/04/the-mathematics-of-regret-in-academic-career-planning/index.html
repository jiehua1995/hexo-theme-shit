Abstract

Here we develop a mathematical framework for quantifying academic career regret, measuring the gap between the career you planned and the career you have, and finding that the mean regret across all career stages is 47%.

Introduction

Every academic has made career decisions that they now regret. The PhD program they chose “because it sounded interesting.” The postdoc they accepted “for the experience.” The university they joined “for the location.”

Results

Mean regret by career stage:

  • PhD student: 12% (still hopeful)
  • Postdoc: 34% (realization setting in)
  • Assistant Professor: 67% (tenure clock is real)
  • Associate Professor: 54% (some regret has been replaced by comfort)
  • Full Professor: 41% (too old to change; also, the view from here is nice)

The “Counterfactual Department” Thought Experiment

We asked 500 academics: “If you could restart your career today, knowing what you know now, would you choose a different path?” 89% said yes. Of those, 67% would choose a different field entirely.

Discussion

Academic career regret is not a personal failure. It is a structural feature of a system in which 22-year-olds are asked to make irreversible decisions about their lives based on incomplete information, vague interests, and the influence of a single professor who said “you should pursue this.”
