How three words can sustain an academic for months

Abstract

Here we present a decade-long longitudinal study tracking the psychological trajectory of authors who receive "minor revision" decisions, documenting the full arc from euphoria to exhaustion to the gradual realization that "minor" is a literary device, not a measurement.

Keywords: minor revision, hope, revision psychology

Introduction

“Minor revision” is the most psychologically complex phrase in academic publishing. It contains within it: the promise of acceptance, the obligation of additional work, the ambiguity of expectations, and the implicit threat that the reviewers may change their minds if given the opportunity.

This paper tracks the psychological trajectory of authors receiving “minor revision” decisions over a 10-year period.

Phase I: Euphoria (Days 0-3)

Upon reading “minor revision,” authors experience a dopamine spike comparable to receiving a promotion, a love confession, or a tax refund. Email drafts to collaborators are composed: “Great news! Minor revisions only!”

This phase is characterized by genuine optimism, premature celebration, and the decision to “quickly knock this revision out over the weekend.”

Phase II: Anxiety (Days 4-14)

The reviewer comments arrive. There are 47 of them. They include requests for “clarification of the underlying theoretical framework” and “a more nuanced discussion of the limitations.”

The author experiences the first tremors of doubt. “Minor” was not a measurement; it was a tone. The reviewer has shifted from “this is interesting” to “convince me again, but differently.”

Phase III: Bargaining (Days 15-30)

The author begins adding supplementary analyses, adding paragraphs, adding figures. The manuscript grows by 40%. The author sends a point-by-point response document that is longer than the original paper.

At this stage, “minor revision” has become “a philosophical examination of everything the author believes about their own work.”

Phase IV: Acceptance (Days 31-60)

The revision is submitted. The author has developed a deep, almost spiritual understanding of the journal’s editorial process and has accepted that “minor” meant “transform this paper into something the reviewers cannot criticize.”

This phase is characterized by calm, resignation, and the decision to never submit to this journal again.

Quantitative Analysis

Tracking 1,247 authors, we find the mean duration of Phase I (euphoria) is 4.2 days. The mean duration of Phase II (anxiety) is 18.7 days. The mean duration of Phase III (bargaining) is 23.4 days. The mean time from “minor revision” to actual acceptance is 67 days, despite the word “minor.”

Discussion

“Minor revision” should be replaced with a more honest terminology:

  • Minor revision → “We will accept this if you do moderate additional work”
  • Major revision → “We will consider this if you rewrite it and we change our minds”
  • Reject and resubmit → “We rejected this, but we liked you enough to offer a second chance, which is its own form of cruelty”
