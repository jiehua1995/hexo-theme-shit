How foliage predicts citation counts

Abstract

Here we report a cross-institutional analysis finding that the density and health of office plants predicts the impact factors of journals where department members publish.

Keywords: office plants, impact factor, institutional metrics

Introduction

It is well established that office aesthetics influence employee morale, productivity, and the rate at which people remember to water their ferns. Less explored is the relationship between office greenery and the prestige of academic output.

We hypothesize that departments with healthier, more numerous office plants publish in higher-impact journals, not because of causation but because both variables are driven by the same latent factor: institutional delusion about the importance of what they are doing.

Methods

We surveyed 234 departments across 18 institutions, recording:

  • Number of living plants per 100 square meters
  • Plant health (thriving, surviving, wilting, spiritually present)
  • Species diversity (monstera, pothos, succulent, “we don’t know what this is but it’s alive”)
  • Departmental impact factor (mean IF of publications over the past 3 years)

Results

A strong positive correlation emerged between plant health and journal impact factor (r = 0.67, p < 0.001). Departments with thriving monstera plants published in journals averaging IF = 8.4, while departments whose only plant was a cactus that “may be dead” published in journals averaging IF = 1.2.

The effect remained significant after controlling for departmental budget, with the plant coefficient remaining robust (β = 0.42, p < 0.001).

The “Green Belief” Mechanism

We propose the “Green Belief” mechanism: offices with plants create an environment where researchers believe their work matters. This belief, in turn, produces prose that sounds more confident, which reviewers interpret as more convincing, which leads to higher-impact publications.

Causality may flow in the opposite direction: departments that publish in high-IF journals have more money for plants. We cannot distinguish between these explanations, which is itself a contribution.

Discussion

Office plants should be recognized as an institutional metric as powerful as any existing indicator of research quality. Funding agencies might consider adding a “greenery audit” to their peer-review process, with plant health weighted equivalently to preliminary data.

One reviewer (unseen by the authors) noted: “If this paper is accepted, I will water my office plant. If it is rejected, I will not. This suggests the study has practical significance.”
