Introduction

Citations are not merely acknowledgments of prior work. They are the primary form of social currency in academia, traded strategically between authors who understand that every reference is both a compliment and a political act.

This paper develops an econometric model of citation as social currency, wherein the act of citing is reframed from scholarly obligation to strategic investment.

The Citation Exchange

We model the citation network as a financial exchange with the following properties:

  • Bid: The act of citing another author, representing a claim of intellectual alignment
  • Ask: The expectation of reciprocal citation, typically 3-5x the bid within 18 months
  • Exchange Rate: Determined by journal impact factor (IF ≥ 10 = premium currency)
  • Laundering: Self-citation, which converts “I think your work is important” into “The literature suggests your work is important”

Citation Arbitrage

Smart authors engage in citation arbitrage: citing a high-prestige author in a low-prestige journal to “upgrade” that journal’s perceived relevance. This strategy yields returns of approximately 0.3 impact-factor points per citation, though it carries the risk of the cited author noticing and feeling awkward.

The Collateral of Citation

Authors who fail to cite a particular colleague face increasing social collateral requirements: their manuscripts may experience “accidental” submission to that colleague’s journal, or their conference presentations may be scheduled directly against the colleague’s keynote.

Empirical Findings

A regression of citation count on the cited author’s h-index, proximity to the citing author’s desk, and the frequency of joint conference attendance yields an R² of 0.89. The only variable that does not predict citation is whether the cited work is actually relevant.

Discussion

Citation networks are best understood as informal credit systems, wherein academic capital is distributed through a complex web of mutual obligation, strategic omission, and occasional genuine appreciation that everyone pretends is purely strategic.
