Abstract

Here we propose that academic confidence obeys a thermodynamic-like relationship wherein author count scales inversely with evidentiary yield.

Introduction

In physical systems, adding energy can produce work. In academic systems, adding authors tends to produce hedging. This paper proposes a quantitative model of the relationship.

The central hypothesis is that confidence behaves like thermal energy: it can be concentrated in a single author or distributed across many, but the total useful work — defined as the capacity to make a statement without qualification — decreases as distribution increases.

The engine reaches maximum efficiency when N = 1 and E = 0. This corresponds to the optimal condition for speculative language: no evidence to contradict the claims.

Empirical observations across 1,247 fictional papers suggest that S_{authorship} increases monotonically with N but evidence quality decreases exponentially, producing a crossover point at approximately N = 3.7 where the paper becomes collectively certain about nothing.

Phase Transitions

At high author counts (N > 10), confidence undergoes a phase transition from “confident but uncertain” to “uncertain but confident.” The paper no longer claims anything definitively, yet every sentence sounds definitive. This is the academic equivalent of superfluidity: the text flows effortlessly past scrutiny with zero evidentiary friction.

Discussion

The thermodynamics of confidence suggest that the most efficient evidence generators are solitary authors working in windowless offices with a strong opinion and a weak literature review.

Institutional policies that promote authorship for breadth may inadvertently optimize for low-evidence, high-verbal-output configurations. We recommend that funding agencies consider author-count penalties calibrated to the inverse of evidentiary yield.
