Abstract

Here we develop a game-theoretic model of first-author negotiations, demonstrating that authorship order is determined not by contribution but by relative institutional seniority, caffeine consumption, and the willingness to threaten withdrawal.

Introduction

Authorship order is the single most consequential — and most poorly understood — variable in academic collaboration. This paper applies game theory to the problem of who gets listed first, second, third, and so on, until the list becomes a form of endurance test.

The Negotiation Game

We model authorship order as a simultaneous-move game with the following payoffs:

  • First author: career advancement, tenure credit, existential validation
  • Last (senior) author: seniority maintenance, grant credit, institutional power
  • Middle authors: the vague satisfaction of “being in there somewhere”

Dominant Strategies

The dominant strategy for junior researchers is to do the work and claim first authorship through a combination of moral entitlement and the credible threat of walking away. The dominant strategy for senior researchers is to claim last authorship on the grounds of “intellectual contribution,” defined as having said “that’s interesting” at some point during the project.

Equilibrium Analysis

We identify three Nash equilibria:

  1. The Meritocratic Equilibrium: first author = most work (rare, occurs in departments that still believe in fairness)
  2. The Seniority Equilibrium: first author = senior person who did least work (most common in well-established departments)
  3. The Exhaustion Equilibrium: authorship determined by who stops arguing first (most common overall)

The Co-First-Author Bargain

When neither party can dominate, the game resolves to co-first authorship, symbolized by a footnote that reads “these authors contributed equally.” This is the academic equivalent of a peace treaty: both sides claim victory; neither side is entirely satisfied.

Discussion

Authorship order is best understood as a proxy for institutional power dynamics, with contribution serving as a post-hoc justification rather than a determining factor. We recommend that journals require authors to submit a “contribution statement” that is truthful rather than ceremonial.
