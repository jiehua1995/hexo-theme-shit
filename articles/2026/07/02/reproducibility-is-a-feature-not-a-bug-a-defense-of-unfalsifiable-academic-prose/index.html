Reproducibility Is a Feature, Not a Bug: A Defense of Unfalsifiable Academic Prose

On the elegance of statements that cannot be wrong

Abstract

Here we argue that the most robust academic prose is deliberately constructed to resist falsification, making it immune to the reproducible-research movement in the same way that a ghost is immune to a security camera.

Keywords: reproducibility, unfalsifiability, academic writing

Introduction

The reproducibility crisis in science is frequently framed as a failure to replicate specific findings. We propose a different diagnosis: the crisis results from attempting to replicate prose that was never designed to be replicated in the first place.

Academic writing has always served multiple functions simultaneously: conveying results, building career capital, soothing reviewer anxiety, and preserving the author’s emotional comfort. The most elegant sentences satisfy all of these requirements by being structured such that they cannot be proven wrong under any conceivable set of observations.

The Architecture of Unfalsifiable Prose

We identify five structural features of unfalsifiable academic prose:

  1. Strategic Ambiguity: “Our findings suggest a potential association…” (i.e., we found nothing and called it a suggestion)
  2. Qualifying Certainty: “It is important to note that these results should be interpreted with caution, although they do point in a meaningful direction…” (i.e., we do not believe our results either)
  3. Mechanistic Post-Hocism: “This effect may be driven by underlying mechanisms that are consistent with existing theory…” (i.e., we do not know the mechanism, but we know it exists)
  4. Citation Shielding: Surrounding a claim with 12 references, thereby distributing the burden of credibility across a committee
  5. The Future-Tense Escape Clause: “Future work will clarify these relationships…” (i.e., this relationship will never be clarified by us)

Empirical Validation

We analyzed 500 abstracts from a high-impact journal and found that the proportion of unfalsifiable statements (as defined above) correlated negatively with actual replicability (r = -0.76). Articles containing more than 60% unfalsifiable prose were subsequently replicated at a rate of 4%, compared to 31% for articles with fewer than 20% unfalsifiable prose.

The Unfalsifiability Index

We propose the Unfalsifiability Index (UI), calculated as:

UI=number of qualifying phrasestotal sentences×100UI = \frac{\text{number of qualifying phrases}}{\text{total sentences}} \times 100UI=total sentencesnumber of qualifying phrases​×100

A UI above 40 indicates prose of sufficient elegance to survive any reviewer comment. A UI above 70 indicates a paper that may be intellectually vacuous but will never be proven wrong.

Discussion

The reproducibility movement would benefit from recognizing that unfalsifiability is not a bug of academic prose but its defining feature. Rather than fighting this tendency, we should formalize it, teach it, and perhaps certify authors who consistently achieve UI > 60 as “Professionally Vague.”
