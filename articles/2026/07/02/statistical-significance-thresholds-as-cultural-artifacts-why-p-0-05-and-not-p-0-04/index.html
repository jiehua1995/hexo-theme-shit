Abstract

Here we trace the cultural history of the p < 0.05 threshold, demonstrating that it exists not because of any mathematical necessity but because a man in 1925 liked the number 1/20 and nobody has had the courage to question him since.

Introduction

The p < 0.05 threshold is the single most influential number in the history of science. It determines which findings are published, which careers are made, and which hypotheses are abandoned. It was chosen because Ronald Fisher thought it was convenient.

This paper examines the cultural artifacts surrounding this threshold and the extraordinary intellectual infrastructure that has been built upon what is essentially a numerical preference expressed in a footnote.

The Fisher Accident

In Statistical Methods for Research Workers (1925), Fisher wrote: “The value for which p = 0.05, or 1 in 20, is 1.96, just the convenient point.”

This was not a proposal for a universal scientific standard. It was a casual observation about a convenient number. Within 50 years, it had become the single gatekeeper of scientific validity, with entire industries built around achieving it and a global academic population conditioned to treat anything above it as intellectually worthless.

The 0.049/0.051 Divide

We document the existence of a real and measurable phenomenon we call “the cliff effect”: papers with p = 0.049 are published in high-impact journals; papers with p = 0.051 are published in none. The substantive difference between these findings is zero. The cultural difference is enormous.

The p-Hacking Industry

The arbitrary threshold has spawned a global industry of p-hacking, wherein researchers engage in creative data analysis until they find the analysis that yields p < 0.05. This includes:

  • Trying multiple dependent variables and reporting only the significant ones
  • Excluding outliers until the p-value drops below the threshold
  • Controlling for covariates that make the effect bigger
  • The “file drawer” problem: not reporting the 47 non-significant analyses

Discussion

The p < 0.05 threshold is a cultural artifact, not a scientific insight. Its continued dominance is a testament to the power of convention over evidence — a irony that is not lost on this paper, which has p-values everywhere and none of them matter because the entire point is that they shouldn’t.
