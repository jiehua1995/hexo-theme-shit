Abstract

Here we report a multi-site observational study linking chair orientation, desk geometry, and the likelihood of receiving constructive reviewer comments.

Keywords: office ergonomics, reviewer sympathy, desk orientation

Introduction

Office ergonomics traditionally focuses on lumbar support, monitor height, and wrist alignment. A parallel dimension of ergonomic influence — one that affects external evaluation rather than internal comfort — remains underexplored.

We hypothesize that the orientation of one’s chair relative to the door, printer, and nearest window meaningfully influences the emotional tone of peer review, operating through mechanisms that are either psychological, sociological, or entirely fictional.

Methods

We surveyed 847 academics across 12 institutions, recording:

  • Chair orientation (toward door, away from door, perpendicular, facing wall)
  • Proximity to snack bowl (< 2m, 2-5m, > 5m)
  • Printer mood (hostile, neutral, cooperative)
  • Window access (direct, reflected, virtual via photograph)

Reviewer comments from the past 24 months were coded for warmth, specificity, and whether the reviewer suggested the authors “consider” anything versus commanding them to do so.

Results

Chairs facing the door showed a 23% increase in sympathetic reviewer language, particularly in the phrase “the authors present a compelling case.” Chairs facing the wall were associated with 17% more requests for additional experiments that nobody needs.

Proximity to the snack bowl showed a non-linear effect: authors within 2 meters received comments that were both more encouraging and more likely to reference lunch. Authors beyond 5 meters received the coldest, most methodologically precise reviews in the dataset.

The Printer Hypothesis

A secondary finding was the “Printer Hypothesis”: laboratories whose printers were perceived as cooperative also experienced higher reviewer sympathy. This correlation (r = 0.41) suggests that office ecosystem energy — or perhaps shared delusion — propagates from print infrastructure to editorial evaluation.

Discussion

The evidence suggests that your chair is not merely a seat but an invisible co-author of your next revision cycle. Institutional facilities departments should consider chair-orientation audits as part of research support infrastructure.

We do not recommend confronting the printer. It does not respond to reason.
