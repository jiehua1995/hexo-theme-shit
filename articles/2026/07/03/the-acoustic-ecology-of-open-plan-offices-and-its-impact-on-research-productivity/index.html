The Acoustic Ecology of Open-Plan Offices and Its Impact on Research Productivity

How fluorescent hum and distant conversations shape scientific thought

Abstract

Here we characterize the acoustic environment of open-plan offices and quantify its impact on research productivity, discovering that the average academic day involves 4.2 hours of partially understanding someone else's conversation.

Keywords: open-plan offices, acoustics, research productivity

Introduction

Open-plan offices are justified by the literature as promoting collaboration, transparency, and spontaneous intellectual exchange. This paper examines a different outcome: the acoustic ecology of these spaces and its systematic impact on deep work.

Methods

We installed sound-recording devices (smartphones on desks) in 47 open-plan offices across 6 institutions, recording for 5 days per office. Audio was transcribed and coded for:

  • Overheard conversations about work
  • Overheard conversations about non-work
  • The percentage of overheard conversations that the listener partially understood but found distracting

Results

The mean open-plan office produced 127 audible conversations per 8-hour day. Of these, 68% were about work-adjacent topics that the listener could partially understand well enough to be distracted but not well enough to benefit from the information.

Productivity was estimated as the number of hours of uninterrupted deep work achievable. The mean was 2.1 hours per day, with the remaining 5.9 hours distributed between active work (1.4h), partially interrupted work (2.1h), and listening to someone describe their research problem to a colleague who already understood it (2.4h).

The “What Were They Talking About?” Effect

The most productivity-damaging acoustic phenomenon was the partial conversation: fragments of sentences that trigger the brain’s linguistic completion machinery, causing involuntary attentional capture. This effect persisted even when participants reported actively trying to ignore the conversation.

Discussion

Open-plan offices should be evaluated not on metrics of collaboration but on metrics of acoustic intrusion. The primary cost of openness is not loss of privacy; it is the continuous, low-grade cognitive load of processing fragments of conversations that are not directed at you but are nonetheless impossible to exclude from your attention.
