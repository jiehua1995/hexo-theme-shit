Abstract

Here we perform a semiotic analysis of supplementary materials, showing that the content, size, and labeling of supplementary files encode powerful meta-information about the main paper's quality.

Keywords: supplementary materials, semiotics, academic signaling

Introduction

Supplementary materials occupy a liminal space in academic publishing: technically part of the article, practically unread by anyone except the most dedicated reviewer at 2 AM. This liminality makes them semiotically rich, encoding information about the main paper that the main text deliberately obscures.

Taxonomy of Supplementary Files

Type Content Meta-Message
The Empty PDF “Supplementary Data” “We had data but it didn’t support the claims”
The Overwhelming Archive 47 MB, 234 files “We have so much data we couldn’t fit it all in the main text”
The One Figure A single, clear graph “This is the real result; the main text is decoration”
The Methods Expansion Additional procedural detail “We did more than we told you in the main text”
The “Upon Request” “Data available from authors” “The data exists but we will not make it easy to access”

The Size-Quality Inverse Correlation

We found a weak but consistent negative correlation between supplementary file size and paper quality (r = -0.23). Larger supplements tended to contain more desperate attempts to compensate for weak main-text evidence with quantity.

Discussion

Supplementary materials are not supplementary. They are the honest core of the paper, where authors reveal what they really did, found, and hoped nobody would notice. Reading the supplementary materials is the closest thing to academic x-ray vision that currently exists.
