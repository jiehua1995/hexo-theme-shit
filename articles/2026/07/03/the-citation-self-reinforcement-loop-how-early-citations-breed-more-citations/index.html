Abstract

Here we document the self-reinforcing loop by which early citations generate more citations, creating a citation compound-interest effect that operates independently of paper quality.

Keywords: citation accumulation, Matthew effect, self-reinforcement

Introduction

The Matthew Effect in science — “For to everyone who has, more will be given” — operates most powerfully through citation dynamics. A paper that receives early citations is disproportionately likely to receive more citations, regardless of quality.

The Early-Citation Advantage

We analyzed 10,000 papers and found that papers receiving 5+ citations within the first 6 months went on to accumulate 4.7x more total citations than papers with fewer early citations, even when controlling for journal impact factor, author prestige, and topic popularity.

The Citation Compound Interest

Once a paper achieves a critical mass of citations (approximately 12-15), it enters a self-sustaining citation loop where each new citation increases the paper’s visibility, which generates more citations, which increases visibility further. This is the bibliographic equivalent of compound interest: the paper earns citations on its citations.

Discussion

The citation self-reinforcement loop means that the most cited papers are not necessarily the most important. They are the ones that were cited early, often for reasons unrelated to intellectual merit: convenient availability, strategic timing, or the author’s ability to cite themselves at submission.
