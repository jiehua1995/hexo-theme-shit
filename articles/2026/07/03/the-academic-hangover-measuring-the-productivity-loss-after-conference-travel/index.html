Three days of networking, two weeks of recovery

Abstract

Here we quantify the "academic hangover": the period of reduced research productivity that follows conference attendance, typically lasting 2-3 weeks and driven by jet lag, exhaustion, and the emotional aftereffects of realizing nobody remembered your presentation.

Keywords: conference travel, productivity, jet lag

Introduction

Academic conferences are celebrated as engines of collaboration and intellectual exchange. What is less discussed is the productivity cost of attending them: the travel, the sleep disruption, the excessive networking, and the emotional letdown of returning to an inbox that has grown by 200 messages while you were pretending to care about someone’s poster.

Methods

We tracked email send times, manuscript revision activity, and self-reported productivity across 342 academics for 6 weeks following conference attendance.

Results

Mean productivity dropped by 67% in the first week post-conference and remained depressed (23% below baseline) for an additional 9 days. The primary drivers were:

  • Jet lag (42% of productivity loss)
  • Email backlog anxiety (28%)
  • The realization that three conversations at the conference have already been forgotten (18%)
  • The expensive tote bag on the desk serving as a reminder of what was supposedly “productive networking” (12%)

Discussion

Conferences represent a net negative for individual researcher productivity, even as they are justified as beneficial for the field. The “academic hangover” should be recognized as a real phenomenon requiring institutional accommodation: reduced teaching loads, deadline extensions, and a moratorium on “urgent” emails for 14 days post-conference.
