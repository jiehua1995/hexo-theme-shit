Abstract

Here we demonstrate that physical proximity at academic conferences strongly predicts citation behavior, suggesting that the global citation network is organized less by intellectual affinity and more by who stood next to you at the coffee station.

Keywords: citation networks, physical distance, geographic bias

Introduction

Citation networks are typically understood as maps of intellectual influence. This paper proposes they are more accurately understood as maps of physical proximity, with intellectual affinity serving as a post-hoc justification for citations that were generated at conference coffee stations.

Methods

We mapped 5,000 citations against conference attendance records, calculating the physical distance between citing and cited authors at the most recent shared conference.

Results

Authors who attended the same conference within 6 months of publication were 3.2x more likely to cite each other than authors who had not shared a conference in the same period. This effect was strongest for:

  • First-authored citations (+47%)
  • Citations of papers published in the past 12 months (+89%)
  • Citations of presentations given as posters (+62%)

The Coffee Station Effect

The strongest predictor of citation behavior was not intellectual alignment, shared methodology, or common keywords. It was whether the citing and cited authors stood within 3 meters of each other at a coffee station. This single variable explained 34% of the variance in the citation network.

Discussion

The geography of citations reveals that intellectual influence is fundamentally spatial. The global citation network is not an abstract map of ideas; it is a physical map of where academics have stood, in person, holding cups of bad coffee, discussing their work with someone who seemed interesting.
