Abstract

Here we argue that the speed at which a scientist changes their mind in the face of new evidence should be recognized as a primary metric of scientific virtue, more important than publication count, citation impact, or even methodological rigor.

Keywords: falsification, being wrong, scientific virtue

Introduction

Science advances by being wrong. The question is not whether you will be wrong (you will) but how quickly you will realize it and update.

The Wrong-Fast Index

We propose the Wrong-Fast Index (WFI), measured as the time between evidence contradicting a belief and the public acknowledgment that the belief was incorrect.

WFI Score Behavior
< 1 week Exemplary. The author has achieved a state of intellectual fluidity.
1-4 weeks Good. The author is engaged in the normal process of reconsideration.
1-3 months Concerning. The author may be attached to their original conclusion.
3-12 months Problematic. The author is beginning to defend the indefensible.
> 1 year Dangerous. The author has likely published a “clarification” that is actually a retraction wearing a tuxedo.

The Cost of Being Wrong Slowly

Scientists who are slow to change their minds incur three costs:

  1. They waste their own time pursuing dead ends
  2. They waste their students’ time pursuing dead ends
  3. They waste the field’s time reading papers defending positions that the data already disproved

Discussion

The academic incentive system rewards certainty, not intellectual flexibility. Papers that confidently defend an incorrect position are more likely to be published than papers that say “we were wrong, here is what the data actually show.” This creates a system that selects for slow-to-change minds and punishes rapid falsification. We recommend that funding agencies and journals create a “Fast Wrong” category for papers that change course quickly and publicly.
