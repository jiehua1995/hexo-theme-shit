Abstract

Here we develop and validate the Academic Imposter Syndrome Index (AIS-I), a 12-item psychometric scale measuring the gap between external academic credentials and internal sense of fraudulent incompetence.

Keywords: imposter syndrome, self-doubt, psychometric scale

Introduction

Imposter syndrome is the persistent inability to believe that one’s success is deserved or legitimately achieved. In academia, it is not a psychological condition; it is the default state.

The AIS-I Scale

The 12-item scale includes:

  1. “I worry that people will discover I don’t actually know what I’m talking about.” (1-5)
  2. “When I receive praise, I assume it’s because they don’t understand my work well enough to criticize it.” (1-5)
  3. “I attribute my success to luck, timing, or being in the right place at the right time.” (1-5)
  4. “I have convinced people I am more competent than I actually am.” (1-5)
  5. “I feel like I’ve tricked everyone into thinking I belong here.” (1-5)

Validation

The AIS-I was validated across 4,847 academics in 12 countries. Results: 97.3% scored in the “severe imposter syndrome” range. This suggests either a spectacularly dysfunctional global academic culture or a scale that measures what everyone already knows: nobody knows what they’re doing, and everyone else knows it too.

Discussion

The near-universal prevalence of imposter syndrome in academia suggests that the field may be uniquely suited to individuals who doubt themselves — precisely the people most likely to do careful, honest, and rigorous work. Imposter syndrome, therefore, is not a bug but a feature of academic selection.
